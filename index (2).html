Project Description

We created grab and go breakfast, lunch and drink mix kits for anyone in need, specifically high school students, using food grown on campus. Our kits are designed to be used by adding hot water to it. They can be made and eaten at school or taken home.

Teacher Reflection

This project really pushed my students to think beyond just completing an assignment and instead focus on solving a real problem that impacts people they see every day. The goal was to find a way to provide food to students in need while removing the stigma, and I was so impressed with how seriously they took that responsibility. They started by creating and analyzing a survey to better understand their peers’ access to food, how many meals they were eating, and where those meals were coming from. From there, they brainstormed multiple solutions and really thought through each one. I loved seeing them wrestle with ideas—not just from a logistical standpoint, but from the perspective of how it might make someone feel. They were able to recognize when something might unintentionally draw attention or not fully meet the need, which showed a level of empathy and maturity that stood out to me.

What I’m most proud of is where they landed. Their final idea was to create “just add water” meal kits using the food they’ve grown in class—it felt like a full-circle moment. From growing food through microgreens, hydroponics, and our garden, to preserving it through freeze drying and dehydration, and then thinking through packaging and preparation, they truly did it all. Watching them take ownership of this process and create something that could genuinely help others on our campus was incredible. This project reminded me why I love teaching food science—it gives students the opportunity to build real skills while also making a real impact.

Project Description

This project advocates for more volunteering, donations, and fundraising for local food banks to improve our overall hunger initiative—giving families food in times of need throughout Louisiana, Mississippi, and West Tennessee. One main goal is to create 10 divisional meal packaging events where students come together to create meal packets distributed to local food pantries and homeless shelters. So far in one division we created about 100 meals in one day to be donated to a local church—but we can do so much more across 10 divisions over multiple communities!

Teacher Reflection

As an adult leader for our school’s Key Club, I have supervised many service projects from water walk fundraisers to spa days at retirement homes. One of my most memorable service projects is the meal packaging creation.

After hosting the meal packaging event, I could see how well my students cooperated to bring it all together and grow their leadership skills while adapting when there were obstacles. I could trust my students in creating decisions, and this event made me realize the impact we as a school club could make—we collected about 160 items and created about 80 meals! I am so proud of these wonderful students.

Later, I learned about the Explore.Act.Tell Challenge through our school’s assistant principal. We have an Albertsons really close to our school, and after looking more into the organization, I found this was a great opportunity to expand our impact on the hunger problem in our communities.

Project Description

Spaghetti Dinner Fundraiser to Help Feed the Hungry

Teacher Reflection

As one of the adult leaders working with the Church of the Holy Spirit Youth Group, I had the privilege of watching the members of the youth group grow as a team and as individuals. In the fall, the youth began working through the curriculum, identifying each other’s strengths and thinking about how their gifts and talents contribute to the larger group. I was moved by their shared perspective of each other and the encouraging words they shared with one another.

The group had lively discussions about how they wanted to help feed the hungry. They decided by consensus that a fundraiser would be the best way to support a local food pantry and a food pantry that extends beyond the local community for a broader reach. They chose Open Cupboard Food Pantry and the Community Food Bank of New Jersey.

The group took ownership of the project from the beginning—deciding what type of fundraiser, how they would organize the event, and how they would each contribute. They stepped out of their comfort zones by announcing the dinner during church services, asking for donations from their local Kings, cooking sauce from scratch, and serving others. They created a restaurant style dinner that mixed parties at a table, which created an atmosphere of lively conversation.

The program had an inspirational impact on me personally. Beyond exceeding their fundraising goal of over $1,600, they created an event that nourished people physically, emotionally, and spiritually. The first statement after the applause was, “Can we do this again next year?” It seems like this may become an annual event.

Project Description

Their ability to connect emotionally with others inspired both students and staff to contribute. I am incredibly proud of the teamwork, compassion, and leadership shown throughout this initiative, and I hope this becomes a tradition our chapter continues in the future.

Project Description

Students launched the Spartan Dare-to-Care Challenge, a creative fundraiser that used teacher dares to motivate donations, raising $1,000 for our community Crop Walk, collecting over 300 food items, and connecting our school garden with community efforts to fight hunger.

Teacher Reflection

This project had a powerful impact on me as an educator and reinforced why student-led service learning is so important. What began as a simple idea to encourage participation in the Crop Walk quickly turned into a schoolwide movement that brought students and staff together in a fun and meaningful way. As teachers began accepting silly dares tied to fundraising goals, the entire campus became energized—creating a unique sense of community as students worked together to support causes they cared about.

Perhaps the most meaningful outcome was the way it expanded our community partnerships. We were introduced to another volunteer organization now planning to support our garden during the summer months, harvesting produce from the Spartan Garden and donating it to Shepherd’s Pantry, ensuring the food we grow continues to benefit families in need even when school is not in session.

This experience reminded me that when students are given the opportunity to lead with creativity and compassion, they can inspire an entire school community. Dare-to-Care did far more than raise funds and collect food—it strengthened our sense of connection and opened the door for continued impact.

Project Description

The Purple and Gold Pantry is a student-led initiative coordinated by the CTE Health Science and Medical Technology: Public Health Pathway at Southwest High School. The pantry’s mission is to address food insecurity and promote health equity by providing preassembled, nutritious meal kits to SHS families in need. Students in the Public Health Pathway coordinate pantry operations, track inventory, promote donation drives, and engage the school community in service-based learning.

Teacher Reflection

The Purple and Gold Pantry has had a profound impact on me as a teacher and advisor—not just as a class project, but as an example of what student leadership and advocacy can look like in action. Conversations began as an effort to address food insecurity on our campus due to Federal program cuts and has evolved into a powerful, student-driven public health initiative.

Through this work, I have watched students move beyond understanding concepts to applying them in meaningful, real-world ways. Conversations have transformed into purposeful action, with students identifying food insecurity not just as an issue, but as a call to serve their peers with empathy and dignity.

Most impactful has been their growth in leadership and advocacy. Students are no longer just participants; they are leaders who use their voices to reduce stigma, raise awareness, and promote equity within our school community. As a teacher and advisor, I have found this experience both rewarding and inspiring. It has shifted my role from teacher to facilitator of student-driven impact, and it continues to remind me that the most meaningful learning happens when students are empowered to lead, advocate, and serve their community.
