Project Description

We created a grab and go breakfast, lunch and drink mix kits for anyone in need, specifically high school students, using food grown on campus. Our drink mix kits are designed to be used by adding hot water to it. They can be made and eaten at school or taken home.

Teacher Reflection

This project really pushed my students to think beyond just completing an assignment, and instead focus on solving a real problem that impacts people they see every day. The goal was to find a way to provide food to students in need while removing the stigma, and I was so impressed with how seriously they took that responsibility. They started by creating and analyzing a survey to better understand their peers’ access to food, how many meals they were eating, and where those meals were coming from. From there, they brainstormed multiple solutions and really thought through each one. I loved seeing them wrestle with ideas like using old lockers or hosting family-style meals in class—not just from a logistical standpoint, but from the perspective of how it might make someone feel. They were able to recognize when something might unintentionally draw attention or not fully meet the need, which showed a level of empathy and maturity that stood out to me.

What I’m most proud of is where they landed. Their final idea of creating “just add water” meal kits using the food they’ve grown in class, felt like a full-circle moment. They were able to take everything we’ve been learning in food science and actually apply it in a meaningful, real-world way. From growing food through microgreens, hydroponics, and our garden, to preserving it through freeze drying and dehydration, and then thinking through packaging and preparation– they truly did it all. It was incredible to watch them take ownership of this process and create something that could genuinely help others on our campus. This project reminded me why I love teaching food science as it gives students the opportunity to build real skills while also making a real impact.

Project Description

This project focuses on advocating for more volunteering, donations, and fundraising for our local food banks to improve our overall hunger initiative. This hunger initiative is to give families food in times of need, throughout the states of Louisiana, Mississippi, and West Tennessee. One main goal is to create 10 divisional meal packaging events where students come together to create meal packets that would be distributed to their local food pantries and homeless shelters. So far in one division, we created about 100 meals in one day to be donated to a local church, but we can do so much more in 10 divisions over multiple communities!

Teacher Reflection

As an adult leader for our school’s Key Club, I have supervised many service projects from water walk fundraisers to spa days at retirement homes. One of my most memorable service projects is the meal packaging creation.

After hosting the meal packaging event, I could see how well my students cooperated to bring it all together and grow their leadership skills while adapting when there were obstacles. I noticed I could trust my students in creating decisions; although there were still times I had to check that everything was planned well. This event also made me realize the impact we as a school club could make in our community because we collected about 160 items and created about 80 meals! I am so proud of these wonderful students and look forward to whatever they seek to accomplish in the future.

Later, I learned about the Explore.Act.Tell Challenge through our school’s assistant principal. We have an Albertsons really close to our school, and after looking more into the organization, I found this was a great opportunity to expand our impact on the Hunger Problem in our communities. In addition, Key Clubber Karmen Cheng’s Governor’s Project is based on the Hunger Initiative: Nourish to Flourish, and the Explore.Act.Tell Challenge would help aid her initiative.

Project Description

Spaghetti Dinner Fundraiser to Help Feed the Hungry

Teacher Reflection

As one of the adult leaders working with the Church of the Holy Spirit Youth Group, I had the privilege of watching the members of the youth group grow as a team and as individuals. In the fall, the youth began working through the curriculum, identifying each other’s strengths and thinking about how their gifts and talents contribute to the larger group. They kept those traits in mind as they moved through organizing their project. I was moved by their shared perspective of each other and the encouraging words they shared with one another.

The group had lively discussions about how they wanted to help feed the hungry. They decided by consensus that a fundraiser would be the best way to support a local food pantry and a food pantry that extends beyond the local community for a broader reach. They chose Open Cupboard Food Pantry and the Community Food Bank of New Jersey, which helps distribute to food banks across the state.

The group took ownership of the project from the beginning. They decided what type of fundraiser, how they would organize the event, what adults they wanted involved in the process, and how they would each contribute. They stepped out of their comfort zones by announcing the dinner during church services, asking for donations from our local Kings, cooking sauce from scratch, and serving others. As a faith based group, they also incorporated their beliefs as a foundation for the project. They chose a prayer that related to the project, and opened every meeting, whether in person or Zoom, with that prayer. They related the goal of their project to their mission to help feed the hungry, and shared Bible stories related to Jesus feeding people. When the church decided at the beginning of 2026 to focus on “feeding the hungry, clothing the naked, and welcoming the stranger,” the youth group decided to extend an invitation to members of the community who use the Open Cupboard Food Pantry. They involved younger children by making placemats with them and included the facts they learned about hunger. They reached out to the food pantry manager and offered 10 free tickets to the spaghetti dinner to be used at the pantry’s discretion. The manager gave the tickets to families they regularly serve. The youth completely understood the importance of welcoming everyone who attended the dinner as equals and to serve every person who came through the doors. They created a restaurant style dinner that mixed parties at a table, which created an atmosphere of lively conversation.

The program had an inspirational impact on me personally. The young people in this group showed leadership and compassion. They each contributed in ways that best suited them. They created an event that brought a community together. The overwhelming response from those who attended was that the dinner was an event that connected people to support a cause. Congregation members met others from the community, a birthday was celebrated, young and old ate at the same table, and the evening ended with hugs from people who were initially strangers. Beyond exceeding their fundraising goal, which was exciting, they created an event that nourished people physically, emotionally, and spiritually. Their enthusiasm was contagious. Personally, one of my favorite parts of the evening happened in the kitchen toward the end of cleaning up, when they learned they raised over $1,600, and the first statement after applause was, “Can we do this again next year?” It seems like this may become an annual event.

Project Description

JPS FCCLA created an initiative that utilizes student donations to create meal kits for the Hands of Hope Food Pantry. We brought our school community together to collect canned goods that would be used in our meal kits. Each meal kit contained donated ingredients that would be needed for the recipe that would be made and meal cards that explained the ingredients and the recipe. These recipes would be nutritious and efficient for families and the meal kits would then be accessible to those that rely on the food pantry. It would be extremely beneficial for families in particular since each meal kit can feed up to a family of four.

Teacher Reflection

During the “Feed and Flourish with FCCLA” project, I had the opportunity to witness not only our FCCLA members, but also our entire school community, come together to support families in need. Students organized an initiative where donated ingredients were assembled into meal kits for the Hands of Hope Food Pantry. Each kit included a recipe card and all of the ingredients needed to prepare a complete meal that could feed a family of up to four, helping provide both nourishment and comfort to members of our community. What stood out most to me throughout this project was the passion and dedication demonstrated by the students involved. In previous years, encouraging widespread participation in community service initiatives has sometimes been challenging. However, this group approached the project differently by sharing their personal “why” during chapter meetings and emphasizing the importance of helping others rather than simply asking for donations. Their ability to connect emotionally with others inspired both students and staff to contribute and support the cause. This project also served as an important reminder that we may never fully know the challenges others are facing. By creating meal kits with thoughtful, family-friendly recipes, students were able to promote healthy meals at home while making a direct impact on local families. I am incredibly proud of the teamwork, compassion, and leadership shown throughout this initiative, and I hope this becomes a tradition our chapter continues in the future as we strive to make a positive difference in our community.

Project Description

The team decided to make lunchboxes for the unhoused. They made a flier that asked people if they were willing to donate a dollar and a description on how it’d be used. Project Description

Students launched the Spartan Dare-to-Care Challenge, a creative fundraiser that used teacher dares to motivate donations, raising $1,000 for our community Crop Walk, collecting over 300 food items, and connecting our school garden with community efforts to fight hunger.

Teacher Reflection

This project had a powerful impact on me as an educator and reinforced why student-led service learning is so important. Watching students take ownership of the Dare-to-Care challenge was incredibly rewarding. What began as a simple idea to encourage participation in the Crop Walk, quickly turned into a schoolwide movement that brought students and staff together in a fun and meaningful way. As teachers began accepting silly dares tied to fundraising goals, the entire campus became energized. It created a unique sense of community as students worked together to support causes they cared about, while staff members stepped outside of their normal roles and showed their willingness to participate alongside them. The laughter, excitement, and shared purpose helped strengthen relationships between students and teachers in a way that traditional classroom experiences rarely do.

Personally, participating in one of the dares myself made the experience even more memorable. It demonstrated to students that we were truly invested in the cause and willing to have fun while supporting our community. Moments like these helped break down barriers between staff and students and created a positive, collaborative environment across campus.

Perhaps the most meaningful outcome of this project was the way it expanded our community partnerships. Through the conversations and connections that grew from Dare-to-Care and the Crop Walk, we were introduced to another volunteer organization that is now planning to support our garden during the summer months. This group will help harvest produce from the Spartan Garden and donate it to Shepherd’s Pantry, ensuring that the food we grow continues to benefit families in need even when school is not in session. Seeing our students’ project spark new partnerships and create ongoing opportunities to serve our community was incredibly meaningful.

This experience reminded me that when students are given the opportunity to lead with creativity and compassion, they can inspire an entire school community. Dare-to-Care did far more than raise funds and collect food—it strengthened our sense of connection, encouraged service, and opened the door for continued impact through our garden and community partnerships.

Project Description

The Purple and Gold Pantry is a student-led initiative coordinated by the Career Technical Education (CTE) Health Science and Medical Technology: Public Health Pathway at Southwest High School. The pantry’s mission is to address food insecurity and promote health equity by providing preassembled, nutritious meal kits to SHS families in need. This public health resource is designed to improve access to nutritious food, strengthen food security, and promote dignity and equity for all students and families. With commitment and compassion, we work to ensure every family receives the support and resources they need. Our vision is to create a lasting, student-centered resource that promotes food security, supports well-being, and strengthens our community. The pantry is housed in the Southwest High School Health Science Center. Each meal kit includes preselected, nonperishable food items that can be combined to create a complete, balanced meal. Students in the Public Health Pathway (SHS HOSA members) coordinate pantry operations, track inventory, promote donation drives, and engage the school community in service-based learning.

Teacher Reflection

The Purple and Gold Pantry has had a profound impact on me as a teacher and advisor, not just as a class project, but as an example of what student leadership and advocacy can look like in action. Conversations began as an effort to address food insecurity on our campus due to Federal program cuts and has evolved into a powerful, student-driven public health initiative that continues to shape how I view teaching, learning, and student potential.

Through this work, I have watched students move beyond understanding concepts to applying them in meaningful, real-world ways. They have developed a deep awareness of the social determinants of health, recognizing how factors like access to nutritious food, economic stability, and community directly affect well-being. Conversations have transformed into purposeful action, with students identifying food insecurity not just as an issue, but as a call to serve their peers with empathy and dignity.

Students have also gained critical skills in organizational management, taking ownership of systems that keep the pantry running effectively. From designing a streamlined request process to managing inventory and coordinating donations, they are learning how to build and sustain a resource that meets real community needs. This level of responsibility has pushed them to think strategically, communicate professionally, and problem-solve collaboratively. Most impactful has been their growth in leadership and advocacy. Students are no longer just participants; they are leaders who use their voices to reduce stigma, raise awareness, and promote equity within our school community.

As a teacher and advisor, I have found this experience both rewarding and inspiring. The Purple and Gold Pantry has reinforced my belief that when students are given authentic opportunities, they rise to meet them and often exceed expectations. It has shifted my role from teacher to facilitator of student-driven impact, and it continues to remind me that the most meaningful learning happens when students are empowered to lead, advocate, and serve their community.
